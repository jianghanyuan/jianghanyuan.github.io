What separates a system that imitates intelligence from one that grows its own is the question that organizes my research. The first kind of system reflects human knowledge back at us with increasing fluency. The second kind acquires structure no human has yet written down. Most of the interesting questions in modern AI sit along the path between those two regimes. That path has a canonical articulation in the [*era of experience*](
https://storage.googleapis.com/deepmind-media/Era-of-Experience%20/The%20Era%20of%20Experience%20Paper.pdf.) by David Silver and Richard Sutton, where agents learn from their own interaction with the world and eventually move past the corpus humans have already produced. My research targets the technical substrate that has to exist before such a transition becomes tractable.

I decompose that substrate into five concrete threads, arranged in the order a learner has to pass through them. First, the architecture that carries emergence. Second, the dynamics that let a model teach itself. Third, the open ended loops that let it keep growing. Fourth, the efficient training and inference that make the rest of the program practical at scale. Fifth, the alignment that travels with capability instead of trailing behind it. The arrangement runs from the computational primitives that hold the model up at the bottom, through the loops that drive its growth in the middle, out to the deployment and trust questions that govern what happens once it is in the world.

I begin from architecture because the capability we read off a large model is shaped by the computational primitives the architecture is built from. Identical parameter counts produce very different learners depending on how attention, recurrence, gating, state, and memory are arranged inside them. Hybrid sequence architectures expose this question with unusual clarity because they place primitives with different inductive biases side by side and let us watch which routes the model actually uses. My contribution at this layer is efficient inference tool that reads *internal signal allocation* directly off a trained model. The tooling lets us compare how well different architectures route prompt local predictive structure into the channels that actually carry it, and it lets us ask whether a given primitive is earning its share of the parameter budget. I take this as a tractable first step toward understanding what existing capacity is already doing, before we layer further capability on top of it.

The second thread studies how a learner can compile its own experience into reusable structure. When an adaptation trace recurs across tasks, the learner can turn it into a *closed loop operator* that future runs invoke instead of rediscovering each time. The harder question is when such compilation actually pays for itself, because every closed loop operator carries risk once the world drifts away from the conditions under which it was learned. Part of my work in this direction builds certificates that bound that tradeoff against the value at stake in a decision, the statistical uncertainty in the operator, and the cost of deploying it. This question shows up most sharply in robotic learning, where [*Moravec's paradox*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moravec%27s_paradox) reminds us that the things humans find effortless, walking, grasping, balancing, recovering from a slip, are the things our models still struggle to acquire, and where every new motor skill has to be earned through interaction with a physical world that gives slow and noisy feedback. I find meta reinforcement learning useful here because it treats the agent's own adaptation procedure as the object being learned, and that framing is the natural bridge between cheap simulated practice and the durable skills a robot needs in the world.

The third thread works toward open ended systems that improve themselves, which I see as the most credible near term path to discovery. The argument runs as follows. Language data alone caps what a model can become, because the model can only learn patterns the corpus already contains. The path past that ceiling runs through agents that act, search, and grow inside loops that never converge on a fixed target. The full version of this vision sits well beyond what one researcher can deliver alone. What I think I can contribute in the near term is groundwork on the loops themselves. I work on curricula whose problem space expands as the agent improves, on skill libraries that grow without forgetting what was learned earlier, and on measurements that tell us when an agent is finding genuinely new structure versus interpolating familiar patterns. I treat *open endedness* as the engine and *discovery* as what the engine eventually produces.

The fourth thread is efficient training and inference, because everything above is gated by what we can afford to run. One observation that organizes my work here is that pretraining can only encode an average match between predictive structure and host capacity, so each individual prompt arrives wanting a different allocation than the trained average. A method that rearranges internal signal at inference, while every weight stays fixed, recovers some of that prompt local fit without paying the cost of finetuning. The work I have done in this direction is meant to function as a deployable building block that scales with model size instead of fighting it. The same instinct, finding the cheapest place in the compute graph where an intervention earns its keep, drives the architectures and training tricks I explore on the training side. The cost of frontier scale experimentation grows faster than the budgets available to most researchers, and the architectures that win the next decade will be the ones whose compute profile makes ambitious training loops feasible at all.

The fifth thread treats alignment as an architectural constraint that travels with capability, instead of an afterthought that follows it once a system is already deployed. Most of my alignment work studies how information flows through the mediating components in a deployed system, which include reward models, classifiers, routers, and role prompts. I ask two paired questions about each such interface. What capability is unreachable because of the interface itself, and what capability is reachable in principle but missed by the finite training the interface received. I am most interested in cases where these failures are masked by fluent output. One example I study is *bounded role adaptation under stipulated rules*, where a model is handed an explicit role with explicit constraints and a user crafts a prompt that elicits the forbidden behavior through fluent surface compliance. Another is *socially situated action*, where the surface words a model emits look correct yet the underlying move the model is committing to is wrong for the context. I treat capability and trust as one design problem, because a learner that scales without that grounding loses the very generality I want it to have.

What pulls me toward this work is that the line between a system which imitates intelligence and one which quietly grows its own is, in the end, the line that separates a tool from a learner. Closing that gap is a project for many researchers across many years. I aim to contribute a few durable pieces of the substrate that work will eventually need. Architectures whose internal allocation we can actually read. Adaptation procedures that compile themselves into reusable form. Training loops that keep finding genuinely new structure. Inference paths that earn the compute they consume. Alignment that is built in from the start. I want my work to take the science as seriously as the engineering, and to treat the systems we are building with the care owed to anything that learns.
